Awakening#

John woke to the sound of the alarm, sharp and precise, cutting cleanly through the quiet of his room.

For a moment, he didn’t move.

Sixteen years of early mornings, of drills until his arms shook, of lectures on lineage, duty, and power. Sixteen years leading to this single day.

He exhaled slowly and sat up.

Today was the Awakening.

Across the continent, nobles and commoners alike would step forward and discover what the world had decided they would become—swordsmen, mages, knights, scholars.

John swung his legs over the side of the bed, the cool floor grounding him. His room was as it had always been—neat, controlled, efficient. Nothing out of place. Nothing unnecessary.

Just like he’d been taught.

Today, that might change.

He stood and began getting ready with practiced efficiency, movements drilled into him over years of repetition. Formal attire. House colors. Every detail mattered. Appearances mattered.

Expectations mattered.

His family wasn’t just wealthy—they were powerful. The kind of power people lowered their heads to without realizing it.

His family was not the most powerful on the continent, not even in the city, but powerful nonetheless.

And he was the heir.

Not the spare. Not a distant cousin.

Him.

He paused briefly at the mirror.

No hesitation showed on his face. It wouldn’t.

But the thought was still there, quiet and persistent:

What if it isn’t enough?

Not weak. Not useless.

Just… not enough.

He pushed the thought aside just as quickly as it came.

That kind of thinking didn’t help. It never had.

Today wasn’t about doubt.

It was about proof.

Proof that the years of preparation meant something. Proof that he could carry the weight of the family name. Proof—to himself most of all—that he belonged at the top.

He turned from the mirror and headed for the door.

The manor was already awake.

Servants moved quietly through the halls, efficient and unobtrusive. Somewhere in the distance, he could hear the faint clink of dishes and the low murmur of voices. Routine. Controlled. Just like everything else.

Just like every other day.

Except it wasn’t.

Today, everything would change. Today his class is going to show if he is going to be a powerful heir or just a figurehead like so many second generation heirs relying on the power of his ancestors.

John quickly joined his mother and father at the dining table.

This was their routine—unchanged for years.

Breakfast together. No matter how busy the day, no matter how important the meetings that followed. It was one of the few constants in a life built on expectations and performance.

The table was already set.

Simple, by noble standards. Efficient. No excess display. That, too, was intentional.

His father sat at the head, posture straight, presence controlled. Even at rest, there was a weight to him—authority worn so long it had become second nature.

His mother sat to the side, composed and calm, though her eyes flicked toward John the moment he entered.

Taking stock.

Always taking stock.

John took his seat without a word.

For a few moments, only the quiet sounds of utensils and porcelain filled the room.

Routine.

Measured.

Controlled.

“You’re up early.”

His mother’s voice was gentle, but there was something beneath it—something watchful.

“I didn’t sleep much,” John replied.

No point pretending otherwise.

His father didn’t look up immediately, finishing his bite before setting his utensils down with deliberate precision.

“You don’t need to sleep to stand still and receive a class,” he said.

Blunt. Practical. As always.

John almost let out a breath at that.

Almost.

“That’s not the point,” his mother said lightly, though her gaze remained on John. “Today matters.”

“It only matters if the result is worth something,” his father replied.

No tension in his voice. Just fact.

Silence settled again, heavier this time.

John continued eating, steady, controlled.

Inside, his thoughts were anything but.

Finally, his father looked at him directly.

“Do you understand what today represents?”

John met his gaze without hesitation.

“Yes.”

“Say it.”

A pause. Not from uncertainty—but from precision.

“It determines my path,” John said. “Whether I have the strength to stand as heir… or rely on the foundation built before me.”

His father watched him for a moment longer, then gave a small nod.

“Good.”

His mother set her utensils down.

“Your class won’t define your worth,” she said, softer now. “It’s only the beginning.”

His father didn’t contradict her.

But he didn’t agree either.

“It defines your starting point,” he said instead. “What you do with it is what matters.”

John nodded once.

That, at least, was something he could work with.

The meal continued in silence after that, but it was no longer empty.

Everything that needed to be said had been said.

When John finally stood, both of them looked at him.

Not as parents.

Not entirely.

As evaluators.

His mother spoke first.

“Whatever happens,” she said, “come back and tell us yourself.”

Not a command.

A request.

His father added, almost as an afterthought:

“Results matter. But so does how you reach them.”

John inclined his head.

“I understand.”

He turned and left the dining hall, his steps steady, measured.

No hesitation.

No outward doubt.

But as he walked the long corridor toward the front of the manor, one thought remained, quieter now—but sharper.

Not fear.

Not uncertainty.

Expectation.

Today would decide everything.

And he intended to make it count.

The trip to the academy was as quick and painless as it always was.

John stepped into the transportation circle, the engraved runes along its edge glowing faintly beneath his feet. For a brief instant, the world seemed to fold inward—light compressing, sound vanishing—

Then it was over.

He stepped out into the school hall, the shift so seamless it barely registered.

Students were already gathering, flowing in a steady stream toward the main auditorium. Voices overlapped into a low hum—anticipation, nerves, excitement. Today wasn’t just important for him.

It was important for all of them.

John moved with the crowd, neither rushing nor lingering, naturally finding his place in line.

Thousands of students filled the hall.

And he couldn’t name one.

Not really.

There were faces he recognized—classmates from lectures, training partners from drills—but they were acquaintances at best. Names without weight. Interactions without meaning.

He had never needed more than that.

His status saw to it.

Among commoners, he was distant by default. Polite, respected… but never approached as an equal. There was always a line—unspoken, but firmly in place.

Among nobles, it was worse.

His family was wealthy. Influential. Rising.

But not established.

Not yet.

To the older houses, lineage mattered more than gold. Time mattered more than recent success. And his family—despite everything they had built—had neither in sufficient measure.

So they watched.

Measured.

Judged.

And John, as heir, carried that judgment with him whether he wanted to or not.

He could feel it now.

Subtle glances. Quiet conversations that dipped just slightly as he passed. Not open hostility—nothing so crude.

Just distance.

Calculated distance.

He didn’t react.

Didn’t slow.

Didn’t acknowledge it in any visible way.

He was used to it.

Had been, for years.

Still, a small part of him noted the irony.

Among thousands of students, on the most important day of their lives—

He stood alone.

The flow of students funneled into the main auditorium, the low hum of conversation gradually swelling into something heavier—anticipation layered over nerves.

John stepped through the wide doors and paused, just for a moment.

The space was vast.

Tiered seating rose in smooth arcs around a central platform, the Awakening Circle etched into the polished stone at its center. Runes lined its edges—older than the academy itself, if the stories were to be believed. Power lingered there, faint but undeniable, like the echo of something vast and distant.

At the far end, faculty and officials stood in quiet clusters, their expressions composed, watchful.

Evaluating.

Always evaluating.

John moved to his assigned section and took his seat.

From here, he had a clear view of the platform.

A clear view of what was coming.

The first student was called.

A boy—nervous, shoulders tight—made his way down to the circle. The hall quieted, conversations tapering off into silence as he stepped into position.

For a brief moment, nothing happened.

Then the runes flared.

Light rose, soft at first, then intensifying until it wrapped around him completely.

A pause.

A breath.

Class: Apprentice Swordsman

A ripple moved through the auditorium.

Not impressive.

Not weak.

Acceptable.

The boy exhaled in visible relief as he stepped off the platform.

The next followed.

And the next.

Class: Elemental Initiate — Fire Affinity Class: Shield Bearer Class: Wind Adept Class: Arcane Scholar

The rhythm established itself quickly.

Call. Walk. Light. Result.

Each class announced in the same measured tone, projected clearly across the hall.

Some drew quiet approval.

Some drew disappointment.

A few—

A few drew attention.

A girl stepped forward, posture steady, gaze forward.

The light flared brighter this time.

Sharper.

Class: Stormblade

This time, the reaction wasn’t subtle.

Murmurs spread immediately—interest, recognition.

A noble house, then. An old one.

John didn’t turn to look.

He didn’t need to.

Time passed.

Slowly.

And quickly.

More names.

More classes.

More futures decided in a single moment.

John watched.

Observed.

Measured.

Patterns emerged.

Powerful classes carried weight in the air itself—denser manifestations, brighter light, longer awakenings. Weaker ones passed almost quietly, as if the world had little to say about them.

He filed it away.

Not emotion.

Not yet.

Just information.

His number drew closer.

He didn’t check it.

Didn’t need to.

He could feel it in the pacing.

In the thinning rows.

In the way his section shifted as names were called and seats emptied.

A boy from one of the older noble houses stepped forward next.

Confident.

Almost relaxed.

The light surged.

Class: High Flame Mage

Approval.

Clear this time.

Faculty nodded. Nobles in the audience leaned forward slightly. That was a class with history. With recognition.

With a future already outlined.

John watched him step down, already surrounded—attention, expectation, opportunity.

That would not be his path.

Another name.

Another result.

Closer.

His pulse remained steady.

His breathing even.

Years of training ensured that much.

But beneath it—

Something tighter had begun to form.

Not fear.

Not quite.

Pressure.

The culmination of sixteen years, narrowing into a single moment.

No second attempts.

No adjustments.

No preparation beyond what had already been done.

Just result.

A pause.

Longer than the others.

Then—

“John.”

The name carried clearly across the hall.

No title attached.

Not here.

Every sound in the auditorium seemed to fall away.

John stood.

Dozens of eyes shifted toward him.

Some curious.

Some indifferent.

Some—sharp with quiet judgment.

He ignored them all.

Step.

Step.

Step.

Each movement measured as he made his way down toward the platform.

The Awakening Circle waited.

Silent.

Ancient.

Unyielding.

He stepped into place.

For the first time since morning—

He allowed himself a single thought.

Let it be enough.

The runes ignited.

John found himself standing in a world of white.

No ground. No sky. No sense of distance—only empty, endless space stretching in every direction.

Then—

Movement.

Far ahead, something began to take shape.

Shadows.

Hundreds of them.

Featureless, shifting forms, packed tightly together like an advancing tide. Silent, but heavy with intent. Watching. Waiting.

Opposing them—

A single figure.

Alone.

Still.

For a brief moment, nothing happened.

The two forces simply faced each other.

Then the man moved.

No—he split.

In the span of a heartbeat, one became many.

Two. Ten. Dozens.

Hundreds.

An entire line of identical figures now stood where one had been, each perfectly still, perfectly aligned.

Each one raised an arm.

In their hands—

Weapons.

Not swords. Not staves. Not anything John had ever been taught to recognize.

Compact. Precise.

But unmistakably—

Dangerous.

There was no chant.

No flare of magic.

No warning.

Just a single, unified motion.

The weapons spoke.

Thunder cracked across the empty white.

Not once—

But hundreds of times at once.

A storm of small, impossibly fast projectiles tore across the distance.

The shadows didn’t advance.

Didn’t react.

They simply collapsed.

One after another, the mass of darkness broke apart, falling, dissolving, erased under relentless, overwhelming force.

Silence returned just as quickly as it had been shattered.

The army of shadows was gone.

Only the figures remained.

Still.

Waiting.

Watching.

And for a brief, impossible moment—

John had the distinct feeling…

They were looking at him.

The white began to fade.

Sound returned first.

A distant murmur.

Then light—

Real light—

The auditorium snapped back into place around him.

The Awakening Circle beneath his feet still glowed faintly, its power settling.

For a fraction of a second, no one spoke.

Then—

Class Awakened: Gunslinger

Silence.

Not the quiet murmur that followed an average result.

Not the restrained approval of a strong one.

This was different.

Confusion rippled outward almost immediately.

Whispers, sharper this time.

Uncertain.

“Gunslinger?” “What is that?” “Is that even a combat class?”

Among the faculty, expressions shifted.

Not impressed.

Not disappointed.

Concerned.

Evaluating something they did not understand.

John stood still in the circle.

Outwardly calm.

Controlled.

But internally—

One thought cut cleanly through everything else.

I’ve never heard of it either.

The system lingered in his vision, faint but undeniable.

Unfamiliar.

Uncategorized.

And completely his.

Now that he has awakened he has access to the system. This will tell him his attributes, and skills,

Name: John Smith
Age: 16
Class: Gunslinger Note can only equip class specific items
Talent: Rapid Regeneration
Attributes:
STR 8
CON 8
DEX 12
INT 10
SPR 12
Skills:
Armory Manufacture Level 1
Heads Up Display Level 1
Clone Level 1
Inventory:
Blueprint Single Action Revolver
Blueprint Holster
Blueprint Basic Ammunition

While John was pondering over his status screen and inventory the world was reacting to his awakening. Scholars and ancient ancestors are pouring over old tombs looking for anything that can point them to what this class is and how strong or weak it may be. The main question on their minds is will the Smith family become a threat or will it be able to be controlled.

When the final name was called and the last glow of the Awakening Circle faded, the hall slowly shifted from ceremony to movement.

Students stood.

Voices rose.

Clusters formed almost immediately—celebration, disappointment, comparison.

John didn’t linger.

He made his way out with the same measured pace he had entered, slipping through the crowd without drawing attention beyond what was already there.

Still—

He could feel it.

Eyes following.

Questions forming.

He stepped into the transportation circle without hesitation.

Light folded.

The world shifted.

And then—

Home.

The manor was quiet.

Not empty—never empty—but waiting.

He didn’t have to ask where they were.

The study.

He moved through the halls with steady steps, each one echoing faintly against polished stone. Servants stepped aside as he passed, subtle but immediate.

They already knew.

Rumors and gossip spread fast among the nobles.

The doors to the study were closed.

He knocked once.

“Enter.”

John stepped inside.

His father stood near the window, hands clasped behind his back, gaze directed outward but not focused on the view.

Waiting.

His mother sat at the table, posture composed, though her attention shifted fully to him the moment he entered.

The door closed behind him with a soft click.

For a moment, no one spoke.

Then—

“What did you receive?”

Direct.

As expected.

“Gunslinger,” John said.

A pause.

Not confusion alone.

Recognition of the problem.

His father turned slightly, enough to look at him fully now.

“Explain.”

“Unknown classification,” John continued. “Exclusive compatibility. I can only equip class-specific items. External equipment is unusable.”

That landed.

His mother’s expression tightened, just slightly.

“And the class provides those items?”

“Blueprints,” John said. “I have to manufacture them myself.”

Another pause.

His father’s gaze sharpened.

“Skills?”

“Armory Manufacture. Heads Up Display. Clone.”

That one lingered.

“Clone?” his mother repeated.

John nodded once.

“I haven’t tested it yet.”

Silence settled again, heavier this time—but not unstable.

Thinking silence.

His father moved from the window, slow, deliberate.

“Your attributes?”

John recited them cleanly.

No embellishment.

No interpretation.

When he finished, his father gave a small nod.

“Solid foundation,” he said. “Dexterity and Spirit aligned with your class, from what we can infer.”

Not praise.

Assessment.

His mother leaned forward slightly.

“And your talent?”

“Rapid Regeneration.”

That broke the rhythm.

Her expression shifted—concern, immediate and unmistakable.

“To what extent?”

“As long as a single cell remains, I cannot die,” John said.

Silence.

This time, it held.

His father studied him more closely now.

“Cost?”

“I feel everything,” John replied. “And larger damage takes longer to recover.”

A slow exhalation from his mother.

Not relief.

But understanding.

“Then you are difficult to kill,” his father said.

John didn’t answer immediately.

“Not impossible,” he said instead.

A faint flicker of approval crossed his father’s expression.

“Good,” he said.

The conversation shifted.

“Your class is unknown,” his father continued. “That creates risk.”

“And opportunity,” John added.

A brief pause.

Then—

His father nodded.

“Yes.”

His mother’s gaze remained steady on him.

“Can you use it effectively?”

John didn’t hesitate.

“I can learn.”

Not confidence.

Not arrogance.

Capability.

That seemed to satisfy her more than any claim of strength would have.

His father turned slightly, already moving into planning.

“You will need materials,” he said. “If your weapons must be manufactured, then supply becomes your foundation.”

John inclined his head.

“Yes.”

“We’ll provide what you need,” his mother added. “Within reason.”

There it was.

Support.

Measured.

Intentional.

His father continued:

“You have one week before the trial dungeon.”

John already knew.

“You will determine your limits before then,” his father said. “Not during.”

A statement.

Not a suggestion.

John nodded.

“Good,” his father said. “Then begin immediately.”

A pause.

Then, quieter—

“Unknown paths are not inherently weak.”

John met his gaze.

“They are simply unproven.”

For the first time since the ceremony—

Something settled.

Not relief.

Not certainty.

But direction.

His mother stood, crossing the room to him.

She adjusted his collar slightly—a small, familiar gesture.

Grounding.

“Be careful,” she said softly.

John gave a single nod.

Then he turned and left the study.

This time, his steps were not just measured.

They were purposeful.

He had a system.

He had a path.

Next he had to make a list of what he is going to need to make his weapons, and then learn how to use them. He is going to need steel, several pounds of it, brass, lead, and leather.

He intuitively knows that in the armory tools will not be needed just the raw materials.

His father simply glanced at the list and passed it to the steward to ensure that the materials and quantities would be here by the next afternoon. He knows that the shopping will be known by the other noble families before the materials arrive. He is not sure what they will think of it, he is not even sure what to think of it.

Dinner that night was quiet. John and his parents had much on their minds. Too much is not known what kind of damage is the material going to make, what kind of damage is the new weapon going to do.

Is this class going to throw off the balance of the noble houses in the capital. There is too much unknown and there is nothing to do but wait. They all know that the other houses are going to be watching John.

John could not sit still following breakfast the next day waiting on the materials to arrive. While waiting, he brought his clone out and inspected it. He did not see how it was going to be useful. It was an exact copy of him, it took a good amount of mental strength to keep it active, it has limited intelligence, it can walk, talk when directly controlled, follow simple instructions. But over all it did not seem to overly useful. The fact that it cannot truly die and can be directly controlled, good for certain situations but day to day use not so much.

When the steward alerted that the materials had arrived and where waiting in the basement training room John nearly jumped out of his clothes and headed directly there. Time to test his skills and see what this Single action revolver, holster, and ammunition is.

He moved quickly but did not run. When he arrived in the training room the materials where there exactly as he ordered them 2 kilograms of steel, enough for 4 revolvers, 100 kilograms of lead, and 50 kilograms of brass enough for around 6000 pieces of ammunition.

With the materials on hand John took them into his personal inventory space, sat down on the ground in a meditation pose, and activated his armory manufacturing skill for the first time.

Upon the activation of the skill John found himself standing in front of a workbench. The workbench was in the center of a large warehouse. John looked around, the warehouse was empty two shelves stood across from the workbench the rest of the warehouse was empty.

John found that in this space he could open his inventory, he brought the blueprints and materials into the warehouse, sat the metals and leather next to the workbench and the blueprints on the workbench. As soon as the blueprints touched the workbench they were absorbed, John found them in his mind, when he thought of them he knew the inn’s and out’s of each part, each assembly, how to properly shape them, how they interact with each other.

If he had designed the revolver himself he would not have better understanding of it. He then brought the steel and a small piece of brass over to the workbench. Thinking of the revolver he could feel a drain on his spirit the metals began to liquify separate off, and began to form the smaller, then the larger parts of the revolver, from the frame, to the cylinder, to the springs, and barrel. The parts then began to fit each to their proper place. John did not know how long had passed before the construction was complete, but he felt drained, as if nearly all of his spiritual energy had been consumed.

Leaving the revolver on the workbench he terminated the skill and began to meditate, attempting to restore his spiritual energy. A single manufacturing session drained him completely, he could only hope that the ammunition manufacturing was not as draining.

An hour later, John felt much better, his spiritual energy completely restored. He once again activated the manufacturing skill and returned to the warehouse space. It was time to begin making the ammunition, he brought over a large piece of lead and brass. The metals began to shape themselves according to the blueprint. The lead absorbed spiritual energy and became something new and pressure fit itself into the brass cylinder. It took about five minutes to make a single round. John calculated that he could make about twelve in an hour and that would completely drain him, it would take an hour to recover then he could make another twelve. He wondered if the clone could help, if it could work at the same pace has him it would be an instant doubling of output.

With a thought his clone appeared next to him in the warehouse space, given its instruction the clone began to make a bullet, five minutes later a ready to fire bullet was placed on the shelf. John was relived, this will double his output pace, the energy seamed be pulled from the clone rather than him.

Ten hours later, two hundred rounds of ammunition, a revolver, and a holster had been produced. John was mentally and physically exhausted. He passed out there on the training room floor. His parents saw him there, checked to make sure he was not in any danger. Once they were sure he would be okay his father picked him up and carried him to his room.

His wristband began to chime and vibrate. He began to shake and smack the alarm, he was not ready to wake up yet, he is still tired from all the exertion yesterday. When he noticed that he was in his bed he wondered how he got here. Mother nature began her morning call and with a sigh he got up and began his morning routine. There is no going back to sleep now.

Breakfast was a quiet affair, his parents where kind enough to allow him to clear the fog of sleep before bombarding him with questions.

“Did you succeed in creating your class weapon?” his father asked.

“I did, and created several charges for it, each use requires a charge, and I can hold six charges in the weapon. It seems that the weapon can be re-charged quickly and there is no need to completely use all the charges prior to recharging, you can simply replace the used charges.”

“You pushed yourself too much yesterday, you must balance rest and work.” his mother said with a worried look in her eyes.

“I did not realize how much creating the weapon and charges took out of me. They are quite draining to make, but it seems like they are not very strong. To gain levels I am sure that I am going to need lots of them. The good thing is that now that they are created I will not be draining spirit in battle.”

“Not very strong huh, I think that once we are done here with breakfast we are going to have to see how these charges perform on a practice target.” Straight to the point as his father usually is, killing the conversation and pushing to finish breakfast to see and do rather than talk.

Once everyone was down in the training room with the target set up John opened his inventory but could not find the pistol. He then opened the warehouse there he found the pistol, holster, and ammo. After buckling on the holster he pulled out some ammo put it into his shirt pocket then pulled out the pistol. His parents looked at the holster ammo and pistol with a sense of curiosity but said nothing.

John looked at the pistol and thought equip, as soon as it was equipped his vision changed. Looking at his mom and dad he could see a skull above their heads. Looking at the target he could see basic information of the target.

Practice Target level 10 HP 1,000,000 of 1,000,000

" Oh that’s nice" John exclaimed

“What happened?” His mother asked

John went on to explain the basic HUD that he had, how he can see the hp level and name of the target, and how he can see skulls above their heads.

“Interesting,” his father said, “an active scan skill, good to have, you should not attack anything you don’t have basic information on.”

John opened the loading gate and half cocked the pistol, loaded in six rounds. When he pointed the gun at the target a small x appeared in his vision as he moved the pistol around the x moved. Taking careful aim he kept the X over the center of the target, when he pulled the trigger though the x moved to the right, the harder he pulled, the more the x seemed to move. The hammer fell, the gun jerked in his hand he almost dropped it, the bullet flew off missing the target entirely.

“Hmm, good speed for a level one projectile, much faster than an arrow, accuracy could use some work though. Not much damage, around 14 looks like a mix of magical and physical damage, no elemental influence in it just raw spirit. Good for low level beasts, should take about 2 charges to kill a level one beast in the starter dungeon. You are going to need to make many, many charges if you wish to reach level 5 in the starter dungeon. Working on your accuracy along the way.” His father quickly analyzed the weapon.

His mother smiled “Most ranged professionals have difficulty hitting the target when first testing their skills, yours being a wholly unique weapon there is no one we can hire to teach you. Practice I am sure you can figure it out.”

With those words his parents left him to his own devices, less than 5 days left to figure out how to hit the target and make enough ammo. Too much to do and too little time to get it all done in, if only he could clone himself.

Thinking about his clone, John sat down and entered the armory. There was his clone dutifully making ammunition, a steady 14 rounds per hour at the rate the clone is working he should have around 2000 rounds ready for the beginner dungeon. He hoped it would be enough, and that he does not burn through too much practicing.
