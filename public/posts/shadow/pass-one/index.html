Chapter One: I Think I Did It Wrong#

Nemo Nikt was a nobody.

Not metaphorically—his name literally meant nobody nobody. His parents had thought it was funny at the time. They were still the sort of people who could laugh at things like that, which was admirable in its own way.

They worked long hours in low-level service jobs that barely covered rent in the outer slums of the capital. It wasn’t a tragic life, exactly. Just a small one. Predictable. Quiet. The kind of life that didn’t draw attention and therefore didn’t invite problems.

Nemo liked that.

He attended the local compulsory school, the kind that existed more to process children into adulthood than to educate them. Most students graduated, failed to awaken a class, and slipped neatly into the same kind of work their parents did. The cycle continued. No one expected anything else.

Nemo certainly didn’t.

A low-wage job, minimal responsibility, quiet nights—ideally as a night clerk somewhere no one talked to him unless absolutely necessary. That was the dream. A good dream. A reasonable dream.

Unfortunately, before that could happen, there was the Awakening Ceremony.

By the gods, he hated crowds.

They were loud, suffocating, full of unnecessary movement and worse—conversation. Even standing at the very back of the auditorium, Nemo could feel the press of people like a physical weight. Voices overlapped. Laughter spiked unpredictably. Someone nearby kept tapping their foot in a rhythm that made no sense.

Almost everyone there knew him.

Almost no one spoke to him.

This arrangement worked well for both parties.

It wasn’t that Nemo disliked people. He just found them… inefficient. When asked a simple question like “How are you?”, the expected answer was not, apparently, a detailed breakdown of physical condition followed by an admission of emotional neutrality. People stopped asking after that.

The ceremony began.

Students were called one by one from the front rows. Each stepped up, placed a hand on the Awakening Crystal, and waited. Most of the time, nothing happened. Occasionally, the crystal flickered and the monitor would announce a class—usually something in the Life category. Useful, stable, respectable.

Not dangerous.

Not complicated.

Exactly the kind of thing Nemo would have preferred.

He didn’t pay much attention. One student blurred into the next. A flicker here, a polite applause there, quiet disappointment everywhere else. Families filtered out as their children finished, their futures decided in a few seconds of dim light.

Eventually, the room emptied.

When Nemo looked up again, only a few teachers, the monitor, and himself remained.

He stood, walked to the stage, and placed his hand on the crystal.

For a moment, nothing happened.

Then the light came.

It wasn’t a flicker. It was a sharp, clean flash that filled the room, bright enough that Nemo instinctively narrowed his eyes. The monitor actually leaned forward, surprise breaking through the practiced neutrality.

“Class…” the monitor began, then paused.

A longer pause.

“…Assassin.”

Silence settled over the room.

“Congratulations,” the monitor added, recovering. “You have awakened a combat class.”

Nemo stared at the crystal for a second longer, then removed his hand.

That’s worse.

Combat classes meant further education. Training. Oversight. Risk. Expectations. The exact opposite of quiet stability. Instead of a small life, he was now on a very official path toward a loud, dangerous one.

He felt his shoulders sink.

There went the night clerk position.

He was told to wait while arrangements were made for his mandatory college entrance examination. Attendance was not optional. Combat-class individuals who refused training were labeled rogues, which came with a list of consequences that were both extensive and unpleasant.

Apparently, being harmless required certification.

By the time Nemo left, the streets were quieter. The walk home was familiar—cracked pavement, dim lights, the distant hum of a city that didn’t notice him and never would.

His parents were still at work.

That was normal.

He slipped into the small space he called his room. It was barely more than a cubby, but it was his. Quiet. Contained. Predictable. He lay back on the bed and opened his status panel with a practiced thought.

The usual information appeared.

Then something new forced its way to the front.

Congratulations You have been chosen as a god.

You and many others have been chosen as gods. You will notice that your stats and skills are significantly stronger than those of the people around you.

Your primary objective is to reach Level 100 and ascend to the divine realm. Your secondary objective is to increase your number of worshipers. Methods are left to your discretion.

Welcome to the Pantheon of Umbraeon.

Nemo blinked.

He read it again.

He let the panel linger in front of him for a few seconds longer, as if it might correct itself out of embarrassment.

It didn’t.

He exhaled slowly and let his head sink deeper into the thin pillow.

“That sounds like a lot of work.”

Godhood implied responsibility. Worshipers implied people. Prayers implied being interrupted at inconvenient times by strangers with problems. Likely many problems. Possibly urgent ones.

It all sounded exhausting.

No, thank you.

Fortunately, there was an obvious solution.

Ignore it.

If he focused on dungeon work—controlled, predictable, moderately dangerous but manageable—he could earn enough to live comfortably. One or two runs a week at appropriate levels would be sufficient. With careful budgeting, he could even move his parents somewhere better. Maybe somewhere quieter.

That would be good.

That would be enough.

As for gods, pantheons, and whatever expectations came with them—they could find someone else. There were “many others,” according to the message. Statistically, at least one of them was probably enthusiastic about the whole thing.

Nemo was not.

He closed the panel.

Tomorrow, he would prepare for the entrance exam. There would be stronger candidates, more ambitious ones—people who wanted to stand out. That worked in his favor.

All he had to do was be competent.

Not exceptional.

Not memorable.

Just enough to pass. Just enough to avoid attention.

He turned onto his side, pulling the thin blanket up slightly.

A quiet life wasn’t impossible.

It just required a bit more effort than expected.
